Client Brief

An Australian-based health supplement brand exploring entry into the Japan market. Required a foundational overview of market size, key local competitors, distribution channels, and consumer behavior to inform a go/no-go decision.

01 — Market Overview

Japan is the world's second-largest health supplement market.

Japan's health food and supplement industry is valued at approximately ¥1.1 trillion (~$7.5B USD) annually, driven by an aging population, strong preventive-health culture, and growing interest in lifestyle medicine. The market has grown steadily post-pandemic, with immunity, gut health, and beauty-from-within categories showing the highest growth rates.

Two regulatory pathways are relevant for overseas brands: FOSHU (Food for Specified Health Uses — government-approved health claims) and FFC (Foods with Functional Claims — self-declared, easier to register). Most overseas brands entering Japan opt for FFC first due to lower regulatory burden and faster time-to-market.

03 — Distribution Channels

Drugstore chains and e-commerce are the primary entry points.

🏥 Drugstore Chains

Matsumoto Kiyoshi, Tsuruha, Sugi Pharmacy — dominant physical retail. Require local distributor partnerships and Japanese labeling. High visibility, but shelf placement is competitive.

🛒 E-commerce (Amazon JP / Rakuten)

Fastest entry route for overseas brands. iHerb has built strong brand recognition for imported supplements. Japanese-language product pages and customer service are essential.

🏪 Convenience Stores (CVS)

7-Eleven, FamilyMart, Lawson carry select supplement lines. Reserved for established brands with proven volume. Not suitable as an entry point.

💻 Direct-to-Consumer (DTC)

Growing segment driven by subscription models. Requires Japanese-language website, local payment options (JP credit cards, convenience store payment), and customer support.

04 — Consumer Insights

Japanese consumers prioritize safety, scientific backing, and trusted sources.

Unlike Western markets where "natural" and "plant-based" positioning drives purchasing, Japanese consumers are highly influenced by clinical evidence, ingredient transparency, and brand heritage. Key purchase drivers include: doctor or pharmacist recommendations, ingredient origin (domestic Japanese ingredients command a premium), and packaging design that communicates quality.

The beauty supplement sub-category (collagen, hyaluronic acid, ceramide) is particularly strong, especially among women in their 30s–50s. Social media influence is growing via Instagram and LINE, though word-of-mouth and printed media still hold significant weight for health products.

05 — Key Takeaways

Recommendations for market entry consideration.

  • 01 Start with e-commerce (Amazon JP or own DTC site) before pursuing drugstore distribution. Lower investment required, faster validation, and growing consumer comfort with online health purchases.
  • 02 Register under the FFC (Foods with Functional Claims) pathway rather than FOSHU. Shorter timeline (60-day notification vs. multi-year FOSHU approval) with sufficient credibility for initial market entry.
  • 03 Invest in Japanese-language content and customer support from day one. Japanese consumers have low tolerance for poor localization — this is table stakes, not optional.
  • 04 Identify a niche that domestic brands underserve. Current gaps include: plant-based protein for non-athletes, stress and sleep formulations using Western herbs, and sustainability-positioned supplements.
  • 05 Budget for a local distributor or Japan-based representative for regulatory navigation, retailer negotiations, and ongoing market monitoring. Remote-only operations significantly limit market penetration speed.
